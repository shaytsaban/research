4. Introduction (Lines 72-82)

Reviewer 1, Comment #1 Reviewer 2, Comment #1 Reviewer 3, Comment #1 Reviewer 4, Comment #1

Against this backdrop, we examine a specific yet economically critical disclosure gap: the systematic reporting of investments in employee happiness. To avoid conceptual ambiguity, it is necessary to establish clear academic boundaries. We distinguish employee happiness from related constructs: while job satisfaction focuses narrowly on task and workplace contentment, employee engagement relates strictly to job involvement, organizational climate describes shared perceptions of policies, and employee well-being often emphasizes physical or psychological health baselines, employee happiness encompasses sustained positive affect and broader life evaluation. Operationally, for firm-level disclosure, we separate employee-happiness investments from general human capital investments (such as standard wages or basic compliance training). We define employee-happiness investments as deliberate organizational expenditures and management practices designed to enhance employee happiness, not only within the workplace but in their broader life circumstances anchored in the universally accepted metric of overall life satisfaction. We adopt life satisfaction-the foundational measure of subjective well-being employed by the United Nations, the OECD, and the World Happiness Report (Helliwell et al., 2024)-rather than narrower transient mood states. This comprehensive metric is highly appropriate for organizational study due to the well-documented "spillover effect" (Judge & Watanabe, 1993; Tenney et al., 2016): the psychosocial quality of work is a dominant domain shaping overall life evaluation. Reciprocally, holistic life satisfaction is a proven causal driver of employee productivity, retention, and subsequent firm performance (Krekel et al., 2019). Therefore, targeted investments in the workplace inevitably manifest in, and are accurately captured by, this broader metric.

10. Section 4.4: Reducing Stakeholder Cynicism with Credible Reporting (Lines 443-453)

Reviewer 1, Comment #6 Reviewer 2, Comment #4

Stakeholder cynicism toward corporate claims about employee investment predictably emerges when observers suspect organizational decoupling... A critical vulnerability in reporting on human capital lies in the reliance on self-reported employee surveys to measure happiness. These surveys are highly susceptible to cultural context, sample selection bias, survey timing, and direct managerial influence, making them prime targets for "greenwashing"-or, in this context, "happiness washing"-and statistical gaming. The credibility gap can be closed by implementing rigorous measurement systems and independent assurance practices that make unsubstantiated claims subject to external verification and economically costly (Perego & Kolk, 2012; Simnett et al, 2009). Specifically, to mitigate the measurement errors and manipulation risks inherent in self-reporting, subjective survey data must be heavily supplemented by objective behavioral data-such as voluntary turnover rates in strategically critical roles, absenteeism, and the utilization rates of mental health or flexible-work programs.

11. Section 5.1 & 5.2: Deepening Institutional Accounting Analysis (Lines 496-544)

Reviewer 1, Comment #4

Under International Accounting Standard (IAS) 38, an intangible asset must meet three criteria... To contextualize this institutionally, consider the boundaries of recognizable expenditures. While the acquisition of an enterprise human resource software platform might be partially capitalized under IAS 38 as a technological asset, the specific, deliberate expenditures required to configure that software to support advanced employee autonomy, flexible scheduling, or well-being tracking cannot be cleanly separated from general operational expenses. These targeted investments fail the identifiability test.

Since balance sheet recognition remains prohibited under current standards, the key accounting question becomes: how can firms disclose decision-useful information about happiness-enhancing investments through alternative channels? This requires navigating the operational boundaries between Management Commentary, the Management Discussion & Analysis (MD&A), sustainability reports, and notes to the financial statements. Because happiness expenditures do not meet the criteria for formal recognition or mandatory notes disclosure under IFRS, they must be situated within the MD&A or integrated sustainability reports, where management provides narrative context for financial results. The primary methodological challenge is reconciling this supplementary narrative with audited financials. First, the historical cost model records expenditure amounts but provides no systematic information about strategic purpose... Formal reconciliation in practice would require firms to map disclosed non-financial happiness investments directly back to the audited Selling, General, and Administrative (SG&A) or Cost of Goods Sold (COGS) expense lines, ensuring that the qualitative claims of investment scale match the audited cash outflows.

13. Section 5.4: Comparative Guidance from Existing Reporting Frameworks (Lines 585-598)

Reviewer 1, Comment #3 Reviewer 2, Comment #3 Reviewer 4, Comment #3

Existing frameworks provide guidance for implementation... However, it is crucial to articulate the marginal information content our framework provides relative to these existing mandates. While the EU's ESRS S1 requires reporting on working conditions and equal treatment, and ISO 30414 details broad human capital metrics (e.g., total workforce costs, general turnover), they largely capture baseline compliance and aggregate labor expenses. Thus, we carefully distinguish between what current frameworks require (baseline compliance data), what they make possible (voluntary sustainability narratives), and what this paper is newly proposing (a rigorous architecture for psychosocial investments). Similarly, SASB and GRI metrics often subsume proactive well-being expenditures under general health and safety or training costs. Our proposed disclosure framework adds significant incremental value by disaggregating and isolating discretionary expenditures specifically targeted at psychosocial support, autonomy, and work-life integration-metrics that are currently obscured or merely renamed as general HR overhead in existing standard practices. By isolating these specific strategic investments and implementing rigorous cohort-tracking capabilities, our framework allows investors to price the difference between a firm merely paying market wages and a firm actively building durable organizational resilience.
