Against this backdrop, we examine a specific yet economically critical disclosure gap: the systematic reporting of investments in employee happiness. To avoid conceptual ambiguity, it is necessary to establish clear academic boundaries. We distinguish employee happiness from related constructs: while job satisfaction focuses narrowly on task and workplace contentment, employee engagement relates strictly to job involvement, organisational climate describes shared perceptions of policies, and employee well-being often emphasises physical or psychological health baselines, employee happiness encompasses sustained positive affect and broader life evaluation. Operationally, for firm-level disclosure, we separate employee-happiness investments from general human capital investments (such as standard wages or basic compliance training). We define employee-happiness investments as deliberate organizational expenditures and management practices designed to enhance employee happiness, not only within the workplace but in their broader life circumstances anchored in the universally accepted metric of overall life satisfaction. We adopt life satisfaction - the foundational measure of subjective well-being employed by the United Nations, the OECD, and the World Happiness Report (Helliwell et al., 2024) - rather than narrower transient mood states. This comprehensive metric is highly appropriate for organisational study due to the well-documented "spillover effect" (Judge & Watanabe, 1993; Tenney et al., 2016): the psychosocial quality of work is a dominant domain shaping overall life evaluation. Reciprocally, holistic life satisfaction is a proven causal driver of employee productivity, retention, and subsequent firm performance (Krekel et al., 2019). Therefore, targeted investments in the workplace inevitably manifest in, and are accurately captured by, this broader metric. These investments encompass work-life flexibility that supports family obligations, financial security programs beyond base compensation, and community-building and social-connection initiatives that address belonging and purpose. This definition explicitly recognizes that overall employee happiness both influences and is influenced by work conditions, and that organizations possess meaningful capacity to affect employee happiness through targeted resource allocation and policy design.

10. Section 4.4: Reducing Stakeholder Cynicism with Credible Reporting (Lines 443-453)

Reviewer 1, Comment #6 Reviewer 2, Comment #4

Stakeholder cynicism toward corporate claims about employee investment predictably emerges when observers suspect organizational decoupling, namely a gap between aspirational public rhetoric and actual internal management practices (Bromley & Powell, 2012; MacLean & Behnam, 2010). Skepticism further intensifies when corporate communications appear disconnected from observable practices (Greenwood et al., 2011; Neves & Caetano, 2009; Skarlicki et al., 2008; Skarmeas & Leonidou, 2013). A critical vulnerability in reporting on human capital lies in the reliance on self-reported employee surveys to measure happiness. These surveys are highly susceptible to cultural context, sample selection bias, survey timing, and direct managerial influence, making them prime targets for "greenwashing" - or, in this context, "happiness washing" - and statistical gaming. The fundamental remedy is adopting data-driven reporting frameworks explicitly designed to distinguish genuine organizational commitment from superficial marketing communications (Christensen et al., 2013; Hawn & Ioannou, 2016). The credibility gap can be closed by implementing rigorous measurement systems and independent assurance practices that make unsubstantiated claims subject to external verification and economically costly (Perego & Kolk, 2012; Simnett et al., 2009). Specifically, to mitigate the measurement errors and manipulation risks inherent in self-reporting, subjective survey data must be heavily supplemented by objective behavioural data - such as voluntary turnover rates in strategically critical roles, absenteeism, and the utilisation rates of mental health or flexible-work programs.

11. Section 5.1 & 5.2: Deepening Institutional Accounting Analysis (Lines 496-544)

Reviewer 1, Comment #4

Under International Accounting Standard (IAS) 38, an intangible asset must meet three criteria: the asset must be identifiable, the company must exercise control over it, and probable future economic benefits must flow to the entity. The asset's cost must also be reliably measurable (International Accounting Standards Board, 2004; 2018). Most expenditures that enhance employee happiness systematically fail these tests, making capitalization impossible under current standards. To contextualise this institutionally, consider the boundaries of recognisable expenditures. While the acquisition of an enterprise human resource software platform might be partially capitalised under IAS 38 as a technological asset, the specific, deliberate expenditures required to configure that software to support advanced employee autonomy, flexible scheduling, or well-being tracking cannot be cleanly separated from general operational expenses. These targeted investments fail the identifiability test.

Since balance sheet recognition remains prohibited under current standards, the key accounting question becomes: how can firms disclose decision-useful information about happiness-enhancing investments through alternative channels? This requires navigating the operational boundaries between Management Commentary, the Management Discussion & Analysis (MD&A), sustainability reports, and notes to the financial statements. Because happiness expenditures do not meet the criteria for formal recognition or mandatory notes disclosure under IFRS, they must be situated within the MD&A or integrated sustainability reports, where management provides narrative context for financial results. The primary methodological challenge is reconciling this supplementary narrative with audited financials. This effort faces three fundamental challenges.

First, the historical cost model records expenditure amounts but provides no systematic information about strategic purpose or implementation quality (Berger & Hann, 2007; Blair & Wallman, 2001; Hope & Thomas, 2008; Wyatt, 2008). Aggregated expense line items obscure whether expenditures represent routine operational costs or strategic capability building investments (Botosan & Stanford, 2005).

Second, without an amortization schedule, there are temporal matching problems (Dichev, 2008; Dichev & Tang, 2008). Moreover, because there is no recognized asset, there is also no disciplined schedule for systematically recognizing economic benefits across multiple reporting periods. Therefore, period expenses reported under mandatory expensing may materially misrepresent underlying economic performance, particularly in years featuring unusually high strategic investment levels (Aboody & Lev, 1998; Hall et al., 1993).

Third, measuring outcome attribution is complex. Happiness-related outcomes emerge in complex interactions involving complementary organizational capabilities including technological infrastructure, process redesign initiatives, and market positioning strategies (Bloom et al., 2012; Ichniowski et al., 1997; Milgrom & Roberts, 1995). Any outcome metric disclosed must therefore be carefully framed to provide investors with relevant performance indicators rather than attempting to precisely isolate and quantify specific causal effects attributable solely to happiness investments (Barney & Wright, 1998; Ittner & Larcker, 2003). Formal reconciliation in practice would require firms to map disclosed non-financial happiness investments directly back to the audited Selling, General, and Administrative (SG&A) or Cost of Goods Sold (COGS) expense lines, ensuring that the qualitative claims of investment scale match the audited cash outflows.

To meaningfully narrow information asymmetry without violating IAS 38's prohibitions, we propose implementing a supplementary, auditable disclosure architecture built on existing narrative reporting frameworks and the evolving standards for sustainability disclosure. Firms should position disclosures within management commentary or sustainability reports with appropriate governance oversight and third-party assurance where feasible. This approach aligns with the IFRS Practice Statement on Management Commentary and with the International Sustainability Standards Board's general requirements for sustainability information (IASB, 2010; IFRS Foundation, 2023). To move beyond conceptual abstraction and provide a parsimonious model integrating signalling theory and risk management, Table 1 details the specific disclosure dimensions, metrics, and reconciliation paths required for implementation.

The disclosure framework includes four categories; each formally reconciled to audited financial statements:

Input and activity metrics. Disaggregate period expenses into specific investment categories (autonomy-supportive training, scheduling systems, mental health infrastructure, equitable pay structures, voice mechanisms) with documented coverage ratios, adoption rates, and manager capability development.

Output and Outcome metrics. Report participation rates, retention in critical roles, the frequency of preventable incidents, voice system utilization, and happiness indicators using validated World Happiness Report frameworks.

Cohort persistence tables. Provide multi-year schedule tracking and adoption patterns, retention outcomes, and performance trajectories, which will effectively substitute for traditional amortization schedules.

Materiality linkage. Articulate connections to productivity growth, risk reduction, and organizational resilience to support valuation and investment decisions.

13. Section 5.4: Comparative Guidance from Existing Reporting Frameworks (Lines 585-598)

Reviewer 1, Comment #3 Reviewer 2, Comment #3 Reviewer 4, Comment #3

Existing frameworks provide guidance for implementation. The International Integrated Reporting Council (IIRC) framework (2013) and Global Reporting Initiative (GRI) standards (2021) address human capital connectivity and well-being indicators adaptable to happiness disclosure. The Sustainability Accounting Standards Board (SASB) identifies industry-specific material metrics supporting peer comparability (IFRS Foundation, 2023; SASB, 2018). The Task Force on Climate-related Financial Disclosures framework (2017) demonstrates how structured disclosure transforms qualitative exposures into decision-useful information, providing a proven template for human capital reporting.

Critically, these established frameworks do not attempt to convert happiness-enhancing investments into recognized balance sheet assets, which would violate IAS 38. Rather, they institutionalize structured transparency around the rationales for strategic spending, implementation quality, and measurable organizational outcomes, precisely the information investors require to meaningfully update prior beliefs about firm-specific growth prospects and risk exposures when balance sheet recognition is infeasible. However, it is crucial to articulate the marginal information content our framework provides relative to these existing mandates. While the EU's ESRS S1 requires reporting on working conditions and equal treatment, and ISO 30414 details broad human capital metrics (e.g., total workforce costs, general turnover), they largely capture baseline compliance and aggregate labour expenses. Thus, we carefully distinguish between what current frameworks require (baseline compliance data), what they make possible (voluntary sustainability narratives), and what this paper is newly proposing (a rigorous architecture for psychosocial investments). Similarly, SASB and GRI metrics often subsume proactive well-being expenditures under general health and safety or training costs. Our proposed disclosure framework adds significant incremental value by disaggregating and isolating discretionary expenditures specifically targeted at psychosocial support, autonomy, and work-life integration - metrics that are currently obscured or merely renamed as general HR overhead in existing standard practices. By isolating these specific strategic investments and implementing rigorous cohort-tracking capabilities, our framework allows investors to price the difference between a firm merely paying market wages and a firm actively building durable organisational resilience.
